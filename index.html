About Me

Travel started as a way for me to step outside of my routine, but it quickly became something much more intentional. What began as short trips and weekend escapes turned into a deeper curiosity about the world — the way people live, the stories behind places, and the small details that often go unnoticed. I’ve always been drawn to visual storytelling, which is why I document my experiences through both photography and video, often inspired by travel creators on Expedia’s YouTube channel. I use tools like Adobe Lightroom to shape the way these moments are shared. Each destination I visit becomes part of a larger narrative, one that reflects not just where I’ve been, but how those places have influenced the way I see and move through the world.

This site is a collection of those experiences — a space where travel is both documented and reflected on. Beyond the visuals, I focus on creating content that feels honest and immersive, whether that’s through vlogs, curated destination pages, or thoughtful travel insights. I often draw inspiration from travel guides and destination breakdowns on Lonely Planet, which help shape how I research and present places. My goal is not just to share where I go, but to create a sense of connection to each place — to highlight moments that feel real, unfiltered, and meaningful.

Destinations

My visit to Tikal was one of the most immersive and visually striking travel experiences I’ve had. Located deep within the dense rainforest of northern Guatemala, the site is surrounded by an expansive jungle ecosystem where ancient stone structures emerge unexpectedly above the treetops. The journey into the park itself already feels like a transition into another world — the roads narrow, the air becomes more humid, and the sounds of wildlife gradually replace the noise of modern life. As I explored the site, I moved between massive ceremonial complexes, open plazas, and long stone pathways that once connected one of the most powerful cities of the ancient Maya civilization. The scale of the architecture is difficult to fully grasp until you are physically there, standing at the base of towering temples that rise far above the canopy. Climbing some of the structures offered panoramic views of uninterrupted green jungle stretching endlessly in every direction, with only the tops of other pyramids visible in the distance. One of the most memorable aspects of the experience was the atmosphere created by the surrounding environment. Early in the morning, a layer of mist often settles over the forest, softening the landscape and giving the ruins a quiet, almost cinematic quality. The soundscape is equally powerful — howler monkeys echo through the trees, birds move overhead in bursts of color and motion, and the rustling of leaves constantly reminds you that the site is still very much alive within its natural setting. Unlike traditional museums or preserved historical sites, Tikal does not feel separated from its environment. Instead, it exists as part of it. Roots grow around stonework, pathways wind through dense vegetation, and the jungle continuously reclaims and surrounds the architecture. This blending of nature and history creates a sense of timelessness that is difficult to replicate elsewhere. Walking through the site felt both grounding and reflective, offering moments of stillness that contrasted with the physical scale and complexity of the ruins. What stayed with me most was not just the visual impact of the temples, but the feeling of being in a place where history, nature, and time are deeply intertwined. It is a destination that encourages you to slow down, observe closely, and appreciate the layers of life that exist within a single landscape.

Vlogs

My trip to Tikal National Park was one of the most unforgettable travel experiences I’ve had. Located deep in the Guatemalan jungle, the site felt like stepping into another world where ancient history and nature exist together. Walking through the ruins, I was surrounded by massive stone temples rising above the treetops, with howler monkeys echoing through the forest in the background.

What stood out most was the scale of everything — the architecture, the silence between sounds, and the feeling of being completely immersed in nature. Climbing one of the temples gave me a full panoramic view of the jungle canopy, stretching endlessly in every direction. It was both peaceful and powerful at the same time.

My time in Antigua felt completely different from Tikal, but just as memorable. The city is full of color, history, and colonial architecture framed by surrounding volcanoes.

One of my favorite parts of exploring Antigua was simply walking without a plan. I would move between markets, cafés, and plazas where daily life and history blend together.
